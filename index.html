    BEng. Mechatronics Engineering

    My journey into engineering and robotics was not planned—it was a series of unexpected turns that shaped me into who I am today.

    I wasn't the most studious student earlier. During my GCE O/Levels, I only studied before exams and had no clear academic direction. I liked math, but I didn't even know what subjects were in the Physical Science stream for A/Levels. Moving into a hostel for A/Levels changed things. After a year of fun, I got serious, worked hard, and secured good results. My goal was simple—get into a government university—but instead, I received the Allama Iqbal Scholarship (100%) to study in Pakistan.

    At that time, I didn't even knew about scholarships like this! I had to choose an engineering field, but with no prior knowledge, I picked one randomly. That's how I ended up in Mechatronics Engineering at NUST, one of Pakistan's top universities.

    And that's where everything changed!

    At NUST, I found my true passion—robotics, automation, and system design. I was drawn to hands-on projects, product development, and real-world problem-solving. While theory classes often felt repetitive, I thrived in practical work, research, and competitions. From working on Modular Self-Reconfigurable Robots (MSRR) to designing motor driver circuits, PCB layouts, and embedded systems, I built my expertise across multiple domains.

    I explored robotics clubs, hackathons, and technical societies, where I learned from peers, mentors, and industry professionals. My interest in space and R&D led me to work on a Chinese lunar project (FLI-ME), where I played a key role in the mechanical design of a part of moon rover system. These experiences pushed me beyond academics, shaping me into someone who enjoys tackling complex engineering challenges.

    Now, I specialize in robotics, automation, and product design, with strong skills in CAD modeling, embedded systems, and prototyping. I'm always looking for cutting-edge projects, especially in space technology and modular robotics. My goal is to contribute to innovative R&D, where I can push boundaries and create real impact.

    From a student who once made random choices, I've become an engineer who chooses challenges with confidence. And I'm just getting started.

    Robotic Arm

    This project involved designing a robotic arm in SolidWorks, analyzing its structural integrity in Ansys, and building a physical prototype. The arm's movements were automated using servo motors and simulated in ROS2 and Gazebo for validation. The combination of CAD modeling, engineering analysis, and robotic simulation ensured precise motion control and structural feasibility, paving the way for further refinements in automation and real-world application.

    4-Armed Pick & Place Robot

    The Multi-Handed Pick and Place Robot is an advanced robotic system designed for efficient warehouse automation, capable of handling multiple objects simultaneously. Featuring a three-floor modular structure, it integrates high-torque motors, precise servo-driven forklifts, and intelligent sensing for seamless object manipulation. The robot navigates using a PID-based line-following mechanism, employing IR and ultrasonic sensors for localization and obstacle detection. Controlled by an Arduino Mega, it utilizes the A* algorithm for optimal path planning, ensuring precise pick-and-place operations. Built with durable materials and advanced 3D printing techniques, this prototype demonstrates an innovative approach to automated warehouse management.

    Ice Dispenser

    The Ice Dispenser Machine was designed in SolidWorks for a test at Rock Taps. This machine automates the controlled dispensing of ice cubes. Ice is loaded at the top, where a vibration mechanism—driven by a back-and-forth motor—ensures smooth movement, preventing blockages. A horizontally placed rotating plate, powered by an azimuthal motor, precisely releases ice cubes one by one into a glass. This design enhances efficiency and consistency in ice dispensing, making it ideal for commercial beverage-serving applications.

    Old Mechanical Pocket Watch

    This project involved designing a mechanical pocket watch using SolidWorks, focusing on intricate gear systems and mechanical movements. Since exact dimensions were unavailable, the components were modeled based on visual references and assumed measurements. The design included multiple gears, a spiral mechanism, and an outer casing, all assembled using SolidWorks constraints like gear mates and coincident mates. The final model successfully replicated the aesthetics and functionality of a traditional mechanical watch, showcasing detailed CAD modeling techniques and assembly precision.

    Electric Mini Generator

    The project involved designing and constructing a mini electric generator to convert mechanical energy into electrical energy. Using a DC motor as the mechanical energy source, the team built the generator from scratch using scrap materials like PVC pipes, copper wires, and permanent magnets. The system incorporated a commutator, armature, and a simplex wave winding configuration to optimize efficiency. The generator was tested to validate energy conversion principles, demonstrating the feasibility of small-scale power generation while highlighting areas for future improvements in efficiency and design.
